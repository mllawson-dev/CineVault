Our Story

A Passion Project
Built in the Dark

CineVault started the way all great things do — quietly, in the dark, with a screen glowing and a story unfolding. What began as a personal list of films scribbled in a notebook evolved into this: a fully interactive cinematic archive powered by live data, built with nothing but curiosity, code, and an unreasonable love for movies.

Every film catalogued here earned its place. These aren't just popular titles — they are works of art that shifted culture, challenged conventions, and proved that storytelling is the most powerful technology humanity has ever invented.

This project was built as part of a Front-End Web Development course, combining HTML, CSS, and JavaScript with real-world API integration to create something that feels less like a school assignment and more like a destination.

How It Works

Built on Real Data,
Driven by Code

CineVault doesn't store movie data locally — it reaches out to the world's largest film database in real time. Here's what's happening under the hood every time you browse, search, or click.

01
🌐

OMDb API Integration

Every poster, rating, plot summary, cast list, and award citation you see is fetched live from the Open Movie Database (OMDb) — a community-built mirror of IMDb data. The same source Hollywood uses.

REST API
02
⚡

Smart Caching

Once a film is fetched, its data is cached in memory for the session. Switching genres, re-sorting, and re-opening modals all happen instantly — no redundant network calls, no waiting.

JavaScript
03
🔍

Universal Search

The search bar connects directly to OMDb's search endpoint, returning up to 12 results for any query. Each result is then enriched with a second API call to fetch its full detail record.

Fetch API
04
🎭

Genre Curation

Each genre tab loads a hand-curated list of IMDb IDs representing the Top 10 films in that category. The IDs are resolved to full detail objects via OMDb, ensuring always-current data.

Curated Data
05
⭐

Watchlist Persistence

Your personal watchlist is saved to the browser's localStorage — meaning it survives page refreshes and browser restarts, no login or server required. Pure client-side persistence.

localStorage
06
🎨

Pure Vanilla Stack

No React. No Vue. No Bootstrap. CineVault is built entirely with semantic HTML5, modern CSS (custom properties, grid, flexbox), and vanilla ES6+ JavaScript — a showcase of the web's native power.

HTML · CSS · JS

Cinema History

A Century of
Moving Images

From the flicker of a kinetoscope to the glow of a 4K OLED screen, cinema has always been humanity's most immersive form of storytelling. Here are the eras that shaped everything we watch today.

1895
– 1927

The Silent Era

1895 — 1927

Cinema was born without a voice, yet somehow it screamed louder than anything before it. The Lumière brothers projected their first film in Paris in 1895 — a 50-second clip of workers leaving a factory that stunned audiences who had never seen moving images. From this seed grew the earliest grammar of film: the cut, the close-up, the iris fade. Directors like D.W. Griffith pioneered narrative technique, while Charlie Chaplin turned physical comedy into poetry.

Metropolis (1927) The General (1926) Nosferatu (1922)
1927
– 1960

The Golden Age of Hollywood

1927 — 1960

Sound arrived in 1927 and remade cinema overnight. The studio system reached its peak — MGM, Paramount, Warner Bros., and RKO manufactured dreams on an industrial scale. Genre was king: the western, the musical, the screwball comedy, the noir thriller. Stars like Humphrey Bogart, Ingrid Bergman, and Jimmy Stewart became cultural icons. Casablanca, Citizen Kane, and Sunset Boulevard were born in this era — films that still define what a "great movie" means.

Casablanca (1942) Citizen Kane (1941) Rear Window (1954)
1960
– 1980

The New Hollywood Revolution

1960 — 1980

The old studio system crumbled and a generation of renegades took over. Inspired by the French New Wave, directors like Coppola, Scorsese, Kubrick, and Altman broke every rule they'd inherited. Films became personal, political, and morally ambiguous. The Godfather redefined the crime epic. 2001: A Space Odyssey expanded what cinema could ask of its audience. This was the decade that proved the director was the true author of a film.

The Godfather (1972) 2001: A Space Odyssey (1968) Goodfellas (1990)
1980
– 2000

The Blockbuster Era

1980 — 2000

Spielberg and Lucas invented the modern blockbuster, and Hollywood was never the same. Films became events. Budgets exploded. Special effects evolved from practical genius to digital revolution. Yet amid the spectacle, auteurs persisted — Tarantino reinvented crime fiction with Pulp Fiction, the Coens dissected the American myth, and Schindler's List reminded us that cinema could bear witness to history's darkest chapters.

Pulp Fiction (1994) Schindler's List (1993) The Matrix (1999)
2000
– Now

The Digital & Global Age

2000 — Present

Digital filmmaking democratised the art form. Christopher Nolan pushed IMAX to its limits. Streaming platforms dissolved the theatrical monopoly. And cinema became truly global — Parasite became the first non-English film to win the Oscar for Best Picture, a long-overdue acknowledgment that great storytelling has no passport. The definition of a "movie" is being rewritten in real time, and the best chapters may still be ahead.

Inception (2010) Parasite (2019) Oppenheimer (2023)
